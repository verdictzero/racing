The problemTwo questions the chart can't answer

Part one of this guide showed how a nested RACI keeps ownership honest at every altitude. But some questions live outside the grid.

“How does this task actually get done?” A Task row tells you who owns it and who does it — not the sequence, the handoffs, the decision points, or what counts as started and finished. That detail is below the chart's resolution, and stuffing it in as more tiers would bury the accountability picture the chart exists to show.

“What's on our plate?” One branch's work is scattered sideways across every tier of every chart — a row here, a step there. No single matrix shows it, because matrices are organized by the work, not by the unit doing it.

So the tool adds two more lenses instead of stretching the chart: flows drill below the deepest tier, and Tasks reads across everything.

The moveThe anchor: “this flow implements that task”

A flow can float free — a tabletop exercise, a what-if. The move that ties the system together is anchoring it to one chart Task row.

chart Task row ⚓ anchor flow ⇒ the flow becomes the continuation of the drill

Once anchored, the flow isn't a separate document that happens to mention the same work — it's the next level down, and context moves across the boundary in both directions:

flows down ↓

The owner rides along

The Task row's cascaded doer becomes each step's inherited owner — shown as a dashed A on any step that doesn't name its own. Exactly the R→A hand-off from part one, continued past the chart's edge.

flows down ↓

Parties pre-fill

The anchor's org ancestry — its division and branch badges — seeds each step's executing party as a dashed default, so the work reaches the right unit before anyone has confirmed a single assignment.

rolls up ↑

Health reports back

The Task row wears a flow-health % chip: every step has an owner and a doer, every handoff names its deliverable. An exec reads flow discipline without ever opening the flow.

And when a step does name its own owner on a different column than the inherited one, that's a branch override — it renders with an amber ring A, warned but never blocked. Same philosophy as part one's gut-check: accountability may jump tracks, but never silently.

Anchors also fail soft: delete the task, the chart, or import a file that doesn't carry it, and the flow simply degrades to standalone. Flow data is never lost to a broken link.

The connective tissueTwo languages everything speaks

The lenses can only line up because they share two vocabularies. Everything else is presentation.

Who — the roster

One org tree behind everything

Directorate → division → branch → team, defined once in the Roster. Chart rows wear org badges from it, flow steps name executing parties from it, and the Tasks view picks a unit from it. Because it's the same tree, “lands on Scanning Branch” means the same thing in all three lenses.

The fine print: free-form charts trade this away — custom columns, no roster links — so their rows never reach the Tasks view. Org work belongs in org charts.

What — the deliverable registry

One name per real-world artifact

“Security memo” or “Test report” is registered once, then referenced everywhere: chart rows declare what they consume and produce at their boundaries; flow handoffs carry it arrow by arrow; a step's inputs and outputs are derived from its arrows, never typed twice.

That single spine is what lets a Tasks work order say not just what arrives but from whom — the registry knows every producer and consumer across all charts and flows.

PayoffWhy three lenses beat one big one

Each lens stays simple because the other two exist.

The drill never dead-ends

Portfolio → … → Task → its steps. The ⤵ caret on a Task row keeps going where the chart stops, without bloating the chart with execution detail.

Context follows the work

Anchored steps inherit their owner and default parties from the chart. Nobody re-keys accountability into the flow — it shows through, dashed, until someone decides otherwise.

Overrides are visible

Moving a step's ownership off the inherited column is allowed — and ringed in amber everywhere it appears. Accountability can jump tracks, but never silently.

Health rolls up

The flow-health chip totals owners, doers, and named handoffs into one % on the chart row. Discipline at the bottom is legible at the top.

No magic arrows

Every handoff names its deliverable, so “then the work moves along” always answers what, exactly, moved. Handoffs without one get flagged.

Every unit gets a run book

The Tasks lens turns both structures into a per-unit work-order list — printable as the handout a branch actually takes into the exercise.

Gut-checkKeeping the three lenses honest

Four habits make the whole system pay off

Anchor flows to the task they implement. A floating flow is fine for a tabletop what-if — but only an anchored one inherits owners, seeds parties, and reports health back to the chart.

Let the inherited owner stand unless the override is deliberate. The amber ring is a question, not an error: did accountability really change hands here?

Name a deliverable on every handoff, a doer and party on every step. That's exactly what the health chip counts — and exactly what makes a Tasks work order worth printing.

Keep org work in org charts. Free-form charts have no roster links, so their rows can never land on a unit's plate. If a team should see it in Tasks, it belongs in an org chart.
